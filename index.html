The Accessibility of Feminist Digital Activism

Kaylah Rouzier

What is Digital Activism?

Digital activism is the utilization of online tools to support a social cause.

These are all methods of digital activism!

What makes digital activism truly impactful is when it is translated to the world offline. Otherwise, it may fall under the category of slacktivism, a term used to describe digital activism that takes place mostly if not completely in the digital world, failing to make an impact in our physical societies (García 2023).

What is Feminist Digital Activism?

One of many subsections of digital activism is feminist digital activism. This subsection in particular is meant to advocate for women's rights across digital spaces in order to make change both on and off screen. Perhaps the most well-known instance of such is the #MeToo movement.

#MeToo

The #MeToo movement is an example of what's been coined hashtag activism, the usage of a hashtag online to bring awareness to a certain social cause. The movement exploded in 2017 when actress Alyssa Milano used the hashtag on Twitter to speak up against the sexual abuse she endured from famous producer Harvey Weinstein. Thousands of tweets were created under the hashtag within just 24 hours of Milano's initial post. These tweets consisted of words of comfort and support for sexual assault victims, as well as many personal accounts from women who have also experienced sexual harassment.

The "horizontal" nature of digital platforms allows for voices that are typically silenced by dominant forces to be heard, especially in the context of women's rights and sexual harassment (Loney-Howes et. al, 2021). Hashtag activism and feminism work hand in hand to tackle predominantly white male centered spaces, such as the internet and physical world, that are filled with their prejudiced views (Liao and Luqiu, 2022).

Despite the reach and impact #MeToo has achieved in favor of feminist causes, there is still a great concern with its accessibility. The movement can be described as pharmakon. Deriving from a Greek word meaning both "remedy" and "poison", pharmakon describes something that encapsulates both creation and constraint. While #MeToo allowed for some women's stories to be amplified, it simultaneously prevented other women from having their voices be heard (Wiens and MacDonald, 2020).

So, who has access to such forms of digital activism?

We'll need to examine this in three layers:

  1. Physical access to digital platforms and technologies that afford digital activism
  2. Influence on consciousness of users that do have physical access to digital activism
  3. Online reach received on methods of digital activism (ex. posts, comments, etc)

By examining the explicit and implicit limitations on the accessibility of feminist digital activism, there will be greater understanding on how this translates to the present and what can be done moving forward.

1. Physical Accessibility

Hashtag activism, along with all other forms of digital activism, exists in the digital sphere. Not everyone has access to the technologies required to access the internet and view or participate in this form of activism. Furthermore, the demographics of individuals unable to access the internet are disproportionately skewed.

In India, for example, hashtags such as #SafecityIndia on Twitter featured pictures of high-class women with access to computers advocating against sexual harassment around the country. However, "over 28% of urban and rural poor women that make up nearly 94% of the unorganized work sector in the country face extreme sexual harassment in their workplace” (Pain, 2020). The "faces" of this movement did not accurately depict the demographic of women that were most affected. Those at an economic disadvantage are prevented from having their voices be heard in feminist digital activist spaces due to lack of resources required to access the internet.

2. Influence on Consciousness

In addition to economic status, the political state of the area also influences the access individuals have to the internet. This is where the impacts on accessibility become more implicit. Assuming one has surpassed the obstacle of physically accessing tools to connect to the internet, they now must make a conscious decision on if they want to exercise that privilege to participate in digital activism. While it seems like they now have full authority to do so, factors such as political climate heavily influence a user's decision.

The #MeToo movement, originating in the US, took the world by storm- but not every country was receptible to the movement. Countries in the Middle East, for instance, experienced less participation online. In the Middle East, the internet is used as a tool to enact political control and censorship. Participating in digital activism is a greater risk for those in the East compared to the West because their political climates are more restrictive and adverse to protest against government or other widely popular groups of power (Kaur, 2020). Overall, women living in less democratic political areas are discouraged from participating in feminist digital activism such as #MeToo. They are less confident that their voices will be heard or make a difference, and expect to receive immense backlash (Lee and Murdie, 2021).

3. Online Reach

Finally, after pushing through these obstacles and limitations to hit "Post", it may seem that the job of the digital activist is a success. However, the final piece in the assessment of accessibility still remains: Will this act gain enough traction? What determines virality and popularity online? There are a couple aspects to consider.

Credibility

Activism online is more likely to garner attention if the source is deemed "credible". This credibility is less based on facts and moreso on fame. The #MeToo movement was popularized when famous actress Alyssa Milano utilized the hashtag to share her story in 2017. However, this was not the first instance of the phrase used in the context of sexual assault. In fact, the term actually originated in 2006 when Tarana Burke used the phrase in a MySpace post. But, due to a lack of notability, among other factors, Burke's post did not gain much traction.

Bias: Algorithmic, User-Based

Burke's creation of the #MeToo movement was not overlooked simply because of her lack of fame. When a topic promoted by a demographic that doesnt fit the majority starts trending, it's eventually overtaken by a demographic deviating less from the majority. Moreover, the most underrepresented demographic on Twitter is middle aged Black women (Chakraborty et. al, 2017). While the initial post was not on Twitter, it isn't unreasonable to suggest that these patterns are applicable to other social media platforms as well. This also supports the story of #MeToo's origins. Despite the movement originating with Burke, a 32-year old Black woman, it only gained attention when Milano and other predominantly White actresses used the phrase years later.

This trend can be explained as a result of both algorithmic and user-based bias. Behind the scenes, algorithms are formed based on data sets that are inherently biased. Computer scientists feed AI bots chunks of text to allow them to make connections between words (Knight, 2016). But, these texts, coming from humans themselves, are filled with biases reflected in the structure of our societies. This means algorithms that determine what's trending may disadvantage women, people of color, and various other marginalized groups that have historically been overlooked.

Users may also explicitly (or implicitly) choose not to interact with posts from accounts that are run by those belonging to a certain demographic. This is often due to unreasonable, predisposed beliefs about those of other identities, which may paint them as "untrustworthy" or "unimportant".

How does this translate to the present?

By examining the limitations on accessibility to feminist digital activism in three parts, we reveal how this form of activism is unable to reach certain demographics of users:

  1. Women of lower classes are unable to physically access the internet and other technologoes that afford participation in feminist digital activism.
  2. Women who have access to internet but live in environments politically unsafe for their identities are less likely to choose to participate in feminist digital activism.
  3. Women whose identities are systemically overlooked experience this same treatment online in feminist digital activist spaces.

Moving forward, there has been a shift in how users collectively go about engaging in digital activism. While Twitter (now X) is still a popular platform for this, over recent years much of the world has turned towards TikTok. Its short-video format and extremely personalized "For You Page" create a greater sense of authenticity within the stories shared and encourage users to come together to post about and support a cause (Abbas et. al 2022, Simões et. al 2023).

Further, Tiktok has garnered and maintained an audience of primarily younger generations, giving them an opportunity to engage in digital actiivst spheres more freely. Hashtags such as #mybodymychoice in Germany and #Feminismo in Portugal have gone viral and engaged audiences within their wide ranges of content, from sharing personal stories to lipsync transitions (Simões et. al, Lünenborg and Backes, 2025).

As much as Tiktok has made a difference in how online users engage in digital activism, the aforementioned limitations on accessibility still remain. The app itself, like the #MeToo movement, can be considered a pharmakon of its own. It's been confirmed that TikTok moderators are advised to block posts from reaching the For You Page based on certain characteristics of the video (Simões et. al). While TikTok allows many users to reach large platforms, instigating action in the digital and real world, it simultaneously constrains certain users from doing so.

So, what now? >>

The first step to combatting these disparities in the world of feminist digital activism is awareness. But of course, this isn't enough. We have a job, a responsibility, to respond to and uplift stories of those who don't match the identity of a White, middle-upper-class, heterosexual feminist. Digital spaces such as social media afford users the ability to respond to others' experiences; we must take advantage of this reachability to interact with overlooked stories we come across (Loney-Howes et. al). This also means making a conscious effort to actively search for content from marginalized individuals. While these may be smaller steps, they are a good starting point for achieving improved accessibility to feminist digital activism for intersectional identities.
